Neuroplasticity

Understanding the principles of neuroplasticity affects people because it shows them that "I can do it". It is the idea that our brains; and our intelligence, are malliable, and that with effort anyone can be 'smart'. The principles of neuroplasticity are simple, the brain can re-wire itself, and it isn't rocket science either. Like highways, if more traffic makes the same trips, then clearly the roads must get bigger to accomodate. Your brain works the same way. If you think the same thoughts, are recall the same information repeatedly, the neural pathways in your brain will grow larger, like the highway. Outside of these commonplace scenarios, where neuroplasticity becomes even more beneficial, is when in individual recieves a serious injury. A stroke or traumatic brain injury survivor is able to rebuild he or her life because the brain is able to reshape itself, it is able to, in response to intrinsic stimuli, rebuild or reorganise its internal structure and connections. This allows those people to recover from injuries like that that would otherwise have massive affects on their lives. Studies show that children taught the principles of neuroplasticity, told that they can all be 'smart' performed better in school, on average, than the control group who were told that we are all 'born with a fixed intelliegence'. If you yourself wanted to engage with the principles of neuroplasticity, there are a few thnigs you can do: It can benefit your learning and your knowledge, as well as the way you see yourself. To engage with it you must challenge your brain, to stimulate the growth of neural pathways. One of the ways to increase neuroplasticity, from a useful resource I found is from Medical news today and it states that partaking in "activites such as making music or learning a second language may help improve your brains neuroplasticity"

Growth mindset:

A growrth mindset is similar, and works arm in arm with neuroplasticity. A growth mindset is the idea than anyone and anything can be improved through dedication and hard work. It is the idea that like I discussed earlier, intelligence and ability are not set in stone, and that with work the brain can change and adjust to whatever is expected from it. Something that surpised me while I was researching a growth mindset is how relevant and important reflection is. I would think it a situation where improving and moving forward is the goal, slowing down and looking back is not something that could help. Howevr my research showed me that looking back can actually help show us the progress we have made, and where we came from, in turn cementing the idea that we are all capable of growth. I will integrate a growth mindset into my own journey and learning plan through the use of reflection, as well as through purposeful and dedicated learning. I know now that if I try, if I study and 'program' with intent and dedication I can be just as 'smart' as the 'gifted' people I look up to. I have learned how important a growth mindset is to the learning process and I will try hard to employ it moving forward.
